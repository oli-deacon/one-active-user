Software used to need a pitch before it could feel legitimate. It needed a market, a category, a growth story, or at least the possibility of becoming a product in the familiar sense.

That frame no longer fits the moment we are in.

More people can now build software that is sharply adapted to their own habits, obligations, and recurring annoyances. The most interesting question is no longer whether that software can scale. The question is whether it removes meaningful friction from a real life.

Personal software is not small because it lacks ambition. It is small because it begins with accuracy. It tries to fit a problem closely enough that the person who made it wants to keep using it.

This changes the standard of success. A tool can be worth building because it saves one person half an hour every week. It can matter because it makes a private ritual easier to maintain. It can earn its keep by making a particular corner of life less jagged.

That is enough. More than enough, sometimes.

The software world has plenty of places that celebrate novelty, scale, and polish. What it has less often is a place that takes narrow usefulness seriously. That is the space One Active User is trying to hold.

The project is simple: document the tools people build for themselves, and document the stories that made those tools necessary.
