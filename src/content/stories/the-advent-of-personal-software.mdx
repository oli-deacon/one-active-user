For most of the history of software, a problem had to be broad enough to build a business around before it was worth solving.

If something annoyed only you, or a handful of people like you, the usual answer was to live with it. Maybe you hacked together a spreadsheet, maybe you repeated the task manually, or you just adapted to whatever mass market tool got closest to solving your problem.

That assumption is starting to break.

Over the last few weeks, building things like Landing Brief, Running Insights, and Kit Check made me reflect: are we entering an era of personal software? Software built not for a market segment, a team, or a company, but for a person. Sometimes just one person.

The reason this matters is not only that building has become easier; it's that the threshold for what is worth building has collapsed.

## The threshold has collapsed

For a long time you needed a big enough problem, enough technical skill, enough patience and, usually, enough people affected by the problem to justify the effort.

If the problem was too small, too personal, or too oddly specific, it did not make the cut. You lived with the friction instead.

That now feels outdated.

With tools like Claude and Codex, the gap between "I wish this existed" and "I made the thing I needed" has become surprisingly short. Not always trivial. Not always elegant. But short enough that problems which would once have remained mild annoyances are now genuinely, easily buildable.

And that changes the economics of effort.

When software was hard to make, only problems at a certain scale deserved a solution. When software becomes easier to make, smaller and more personal problems start to qualify. Things that once stayed as workarounds or low-grade frustrations begin to look like software.

We may be moving into a world where the minimum viable audience for a piece of software is one active user.

That is not a joke. I think it is one of the more interesting things happening in software right now.

## The examples are the point

This became clearer to me through a few things I have built recently.

Landing Brief came from a very specific kind of friction. I travel regularly for my work and always have the same questions for each destination: easiest way from the airport to the CBD, can I drink the water, do I need cash or can I just use a card, what are handy phrases, and what is the business etiquette. I wanted travel information assembled in a way that was useful to me. Not generic travel content. Not another search result. A practical briefing shaped around how I actually think when I land somewhere new.

In another era, I probably would have accepted the manual overhead as the cost of doing the thing. Instead, I built a tool.

Landing Brief is a small tool built around the way I think about arriving in a place.

Running Insights came from a different itch. There's no shortage of fitness software in the world. That wasn't the problem. The problem was that none of it answered the questions I actually had. I wanted to know whether my easy days were genuinely easy, how my pace shifted across different conditions, and whether I was actually improving or just logging kilometres. So I built a view over my own data that answered those questions in a way that felt natural to me.

Not more data. Just a better fit for my needs.

Running Insights is a different-shaped view of my own running data.

Kit Check sits in the same category. I live in Melbourne, a city that can experience four seasons in a single hour, and knowing what to wear for an early morning run is genuinely harder than it should be. So I built a small tool that fetches the weather, applies my kit rules, and sends me a notification the night before. It took a few hours to build. I haven't thought about it since.

But that is exactly the point. A lot of valuable software is like this: quietly useful, specific, unlikely to justify itself in a pitch deck, and entirely worth having anyway.

None of these started as startup ideas. They started as points of friction.

That feels important, because I think we are going to see more software built this way. Not as products looking for market fit, but as tools made in response to a real and specific annoyance, curiosity, or need.

## From consumption to authorship

What is changing is that more people can now shape the structure and experience itself. That is a different relationship to software: moving from consumption to creative control.

You do not just choose a workflow. You make one.

You don't just tolerate a tool's worldview. You encode your own.

You don't fit yourself to the product. You increasingly make the product fit you.

That feels like a subtle but meaningful redistribution of agency. And, maybe more than that, it is fun.

One of the striking things about building with Claude and Codex is not simply that it lowers the barrier to entry. It also lowers the emotional cost of making things. The work feels lighter, more conversational, and more iterative. There is less staring at a blank page and more shaping, testing, and refining.

There is more momentum, and that changes what gets built.

When building is punishing, only the most urgent or commercially promising ideas make it through. When building is lighter, a whole class of smaller, stranger, more personal ideas gets a chance. DIY software.

That seems good for software, and probably good for people too.

## What I think is changing

I do not think this means everyone becomes a software engineer. The barrier hasn't gone, but it has shifted from technical skill to something closer to clear thinking and an ability to describe a problem precisely. That feels like progress.

I do think it means many more people become capable of making software when they need it. That is a different proposition, and a more interesting one.

Software starts to look less like a specialised industrial activity and more like a normal creative and practical medium. Not universal. Not effortless. Not always the right answer. But normal in the way that spreadsheets became normal, or slide decks, or lightweight scripting, or publishing on the web.

A thing you can reach for when you want to think through a problem properly.

If that is right, then software expands downward into everyday life. There will be more software for hobbies, households, personal data, odd routines, niche workflows, and temporary needs. Some of it will be disposable. Some of it will become essential. Some of it will only ever matter to one person.

And that will be enough.

That is the bit I find most exciting. Not just that more software can be made. Not just that it can be made faster. But that more people can now make the exact thing they need.

Recent projects have made that feel tangible to me. Each one started from a familiar place: here is a small but persistent friction, here is something I wish existed, here is a shape of tool that would make this easier.

Some of the most useful software in the next few years may never have more than one active user. Not because it failed to spread. Because that was never the point.

The point was that someone noticed a friction in their own life, had the means to do something about it, and built the exact thing they needed.

## One active user can be enough

I find the "one active user" idea strangely freeing.

There is no need to reverse-engineer a broad market for something that begins with a personal need. No need to inflate a practical little solution into a category-defining product vision.

If a piece of software has one active user, and that user keeps using it because it makes part of their life easier, that can be enough.

Some personal software will remain personal. Some will spread. Some will resonate with more people than expected. But that does not need to be the test at the beginning.

The first question can simply be: did this reduce friction for the person who needed it?

That is a healthy standard. It gives us permission to build software that is a little weird, a little narrow, and a little bespoke. Software that might never make sense as a company, but makes perfect sense as a tool.

That feels new.

And, honestly, pretty joyful.
